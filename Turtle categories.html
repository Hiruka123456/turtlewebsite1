Turtle Categories

There are five types of turtles that you can see in Sri Lanka

  • The Green Turtle
  • Loggerhead Turtle
  • Hawksbill Turtle
  • Olive Ridley Turtle
  • Leatherback Turtle

    The Green Turtle

    One of the biggest sea turtles and the sole
    herbivore among the several kinds is the green turtle. The name
    "green turtle" refers to the greenish hue of the turtles' fat
    and cartilage, not their shells. They are mainly found in seas
    that are tropical or subtropical. Like other sea turtles, it
    travels great distances to get to its feeding areas and nesting
    beaches. Females lay their eggs on the exact same beach where
    they were hatched.

    Loggerhead Turtle

    The biggest hard-shelled turtle in the world is the loggerhead
    sea turtle. Their huge heads, which support strong jaw muscles
    and enable them to crush hard-shelled food like clams and sea
    urchins, are the reason they got their moniker. Compared to
    other sea turtles, loggerheads are less likely to be killed
    for their flesh or shell.The Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian
    oceans, as well as the Mediterranean Sea, are home to loggerhead
    sea turtles. Saltwater and estuarine settings are where it
    spends the most of its life. On their shells, loggerhead turtles
    carry colonies of tiny plants and animals that act as vital
    habitats in and of themselves. On a single loggerhead turtle,
    up to 100 different types of animals and plants have been found
    to coexist.

    Hawksbill Turtle

    The Cheloniidae family of marine turtles includes
    the critically endangered hawksbill turtle. In the genus Eretmochelys,
    it is the sole surviving species. The species is found across the
    world, although its range is mostly restricted to tropical and
    subtropical marine and estuarine ecosystems1. All of the world's
    main seas have tropical and subtropical waters where hawksbill
    sea turtles can be found. They are sought after for their
    eye-catching shell.The Pacific, Indian, and Atlantic Oceans'
    tropical coral reefs are where hawksbill sea turtles spend the
    majority of their lives. They frequently spend the day napping
    in the caves and ledges on and around these reefs. They live in
    a variety of settings because to their high level of migration,
    including the open ocean, lagoons, and even mangrove swamps in
    estuaries. Throughout their life cycle, hawksbill turtles use a
    range of habitats, although they are most commonly found in
    nearshore foraging grounds, particularly healthy coral reef ecosystems.
    Large hawksbill populations have been discovered in mangrove
    estuaries in the Eastern Pacific. Coral reefs, rocky terrain,
    lagoons, and shallow coastal locations are where you'll most
    often find them. Additionally, mangrove-lined bays and estuaries
    contain them.

    Olive Ridley Turtle

    The smallest and most prevalent marine turtle in the world is the olive ridley (Lepidochelys olivacea). They weigh less than 50 kg and can reach a height of 80 cm. Olive ridley turtles have a heart-shaped carapace (upper shell) with 5 to 9 pairs of scutes and have an olive/grayish green color. An olive ridley's four flippers each have one or two claws. Olive ridleys come in a variety of sizes and shapes, with the biggest animals being found in West Africa. In the warm seas of the Pacific, Atlantic, and Indian oceans, olive ridley turtles can be found.The South Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans have tropical and warm-temperate zones where olive ridley turtles may be found. They have been discovered to live in coastal regions, including bays and estuaries, while being mostly pelagic. Olive ridleys may travel thousands of kilometers between their coastal breeding areas and pelagic feeding habitats. The balmy tropical seas of the Pacific Ocean are where you may see Olive Ridley turtles most frequently. They may also be found in the Indian Ocean and the Southern Atlantic Oceans. Olive ridley turtle breeding beaches are disappearing as a result of coastal development and increasing sea levels brought on by climate change. Dry sand that is suited for successful hatching might be completely lost as a result of shoreline hardening or armoring (such as seawalls).

    Leatherback Turtle

    The largest extant turtle and fourth-heaviest contemporary reptile after three crocodilians is the leatherback sea turtle (Dermochelys coriacea). It may weigh more than 900 kg and reach a length of up to 2 meters. Only leatherback sea turtles are known to be born without a hard shell. Instead, skin and greasy flesh cover its carapace. With the exception of the polar areas, leatherback turtles may be found in all oceans.Although it is well known that leatherback turtles eat jellyfish, they also consume other soft-bodied creatures including sea squirts and salps. In quest of food, leatherbacks have been observed diving more than 1,000 meters below the surface. In the wild, leatherback turtles can live up to 45 years.Numerous risks to leatherback turtles include habitat degradation, poaching of eggs and adults, entrapment in fishing gear, and consumption of marine trash.
